INSIGHT 01

Urban Medicaid Acceptance Is Effectively Zero — Micropolitan Counties Are 10× Higher

Only 1.3% of urban California providers accept Medicaid — functionally nonexistent for 38.8 million people. Micropolitan counties reach 13.0%.

Insurance Acceptance Medicaid Acceptance Sliding Scale
Micropolitan counties are 10× more likely to accept Medicaid (13.0% vs 1.3%), likely because rural-market therapists cannot survive on self-pay alone. Sliding-scale availability partially compensates in non-urban areas (~51%) but not urban ones (37%). For the 38.8 million people in urban California, the public insurance pathway to mental health care is statistically nonexistent.
INSIGHT 02

Fees Are High Everywhere — But Rural Counties Pay the Most

Rural counties charge the highest median fee ($167.50) despite having $11,500 less in disposable income than urban counties.

California's median session fee ($150) runs 15% above the national median ($130). The burden falls hardest on non-urban households, who spend 4.9% of annual income on 10 sessions versus 3.8% in urban areas — despite earning less.
INSIGHT 03

In the Most Constrained Counties, 10 Sessions Cost Nearly 10% of Annual Income

Sierra County leads the burden curve at 9.7%. Counties below $25K disposable income consistently spend 6–10% on 10 sessions. Hover over any dot to see the county name and exact figures.

Urban Micropolitan Rural
Wealthy coastal counties stay below 3% burden while the most constrained counties — predominantly inland and lower-income — spend up to 10% of annual earnings on just 10 sessions. The fee floor creates a barrier that income alone cannot overcome.
INSIGHT 04

Access Deserts Cluster in the Northern Interior and Central Valley

The lowest-AVI counties — Yuba (16.0), Amador (38.1), Trinity and Lake (both 38.4) — are concentrated in the Northern Interior and Sierra Nevada foothills. The highest-burden counties by AFI — Sierra (9.7%), Monterey (8.8%), Imperial (7.2%) — span inland agricultural and coastal rural areas where poverty is high and providers are few.
INSIGHT 05

What This Means for Policy and Practice

01

The Urban Medicaid Crisis

Nearly 39 million Californians live in urban counties where Medicaid-accepting therapists are statistically nonexistent (1.3%). Medi-Cal rate reform would disproportionately benefit this population.

38.8M affectedMedi-Cal rate reform
02

The Rural Fee Paradox

Rural counties charge the highest fees despite the lowest incomes. Workforce incentives targeting rural markets need to address both provider supply and fee accessibility simultaneously.

$167.50 median rural fee4.9% income burden
03

Access Deserts Are Geographic, Not Random

The Northern Interior and Central Valley show the lowest AVI scores. These areas overlap with agricultural communities, high poverty rates, and limited transportation infrastructure.

AVI ≤ 40 in desert countiesProvider shortage areas
Any change in Medi-Cal reimbursement policy would disproportionately affect the 39 million urban residents currently locked out of Medicaid-accepting care — making these findings directly relevant to workforce-planning bodies and state behavioral health agencies.
DATA & LIMITATIONS

About This Analysis

Source: Two Dimensions of Access dataset (OSF, v1.0, Jan 2026), drawing provider data from Psychology Today's public directory for 57 of California's 58 counties.

Exclusion: Modoc County (population 8,491; micropolitan) returned no listed providers and is excluded from all analyses. This directory captures listed outpatient therapists but excludes community health centers, unlisted providers, psychiatrists, and inpatient facilities.

Caution: Counties with few observations may produce unstable estimates. Findings describe the listed-provider landscape, not total system capacity.
